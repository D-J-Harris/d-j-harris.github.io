# Getting the Ball Rolling

I thought I'd start my posting with a short article about my plans for this site, and some practices I'm going to try
and hold myself to. I've been sitting on this empty site for a few weeks now; maybe it's coursework getting in the way,
maybe it's for fear of getting my words down and out there in the world... either way, baby steps...

## Let's Talk Style

I want to avoid calling this a blog, but as much as I deny it, I've certainly jumped on a popular trend here. I'm sure
it will evolve over time as my interests develop and my expertise grows, however for now I want to make this a space for
me to digest what I'm learning, and in particular, reading - through turning dense material into something manageable, 
hopefully you can learn something, and in turn I learn through teaching!

By this thinking, I have called this site 'Dan's Deep Bites'; playing on Deep Learning and my name, but more importantly
emphasising that these posts are designed to be easy to read, and enough to get you through 'til dinner. I aim to get
myself through the backlog of papers I keep promising myself I'll read, creating short and (hopefully!) frequent posts
about interesting areas in the field of machine learning. This is likely to take a turn towards natural language
processing (NLP) and theoretical machine learning, but we'll see...

In addition, I plan to post more infrequent deeper dives on a smattering of these posts (or 'Bites'), which I've 
humorously called 'Monthly Mouthfuls'. This will be a segment where I synthesis interesting ideas into code, which 
you can follow to learn how it might be implemented. 

## Being a Good Little Scientist

Particularly for these monthly mouthful pieces, but also as a general topic to make an easy and important first post on,
I wanted to bring a spotlight on good coding practices in machine learning. On 8th April 2020,
[Papers with Code](https://paperswithcode.com/)
released an
[ML Code Completion Checklist](https://medium.com/paperswithcode/ml-code-completeness-checklist-e9127b168501),
which outlines five important aspects towards making a repository accessible, reproducible and clear. This has been
endorsed by Facebook AI Research (FAIR) and will form a part of the official NeurIPS 2020 code submission process, so is 
probably pretty important...

I will summarise here the five points. Consider it my pledge for future work, both on this site and personally, and
repentance for any previously committed and pushed sins...

+ Dependencies - I will include instructions for how an environment should be set up
+ Training Scripts - I will include scripts using which models can be trained and fit
+ Evaluation Scripts - I will include scripts using which experiments can be run and evaluated
+ Pretrained Models - I will provide free access to pretrained model weights
+ Results - I will clearly outline results, and include scripts to reproduce these

I like nothing more than easy to follow explanations and code, devoid of any needless extravagance in an effort to sound
smarter. Hopefully you agree, and I can provide this for interesting snippets of research from the machine learning
community.

This effort in coding practices comes as an extension to a push by FAIR towards reproducibility of results. This more broad,
research-oriented
[checklist](https://ai.facebook.com/blog/how-the-ai-community-can-get-serious-about-reproducibility/)
includes further points, such as clear descriptions of algorithms and models, links to data and source code, and
descriptions of how experiments are run. While not directly relevant to short reviews of literature, I will also attempt
to make clear, almost layman-level explanations of concepts, to make the Bites more digestible...
